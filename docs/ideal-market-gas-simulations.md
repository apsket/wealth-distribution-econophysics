# Simulation of "Ideal Gas" (Zero-Intelligence) Economy

Across literature, different rules for agent interaction have been considered. Some models fix the transferred amount in each interaction to a constant $\Delta m = c_0$ while others assign a random fraction of the average amount of money per agent $\Delta m = \nu M/N$ or a random fraction of the average money between the two interacting agents [1]. Each of these choices reflects the economic dynamics differently , i.e. all agents selling their products for the same price or an economy with a range of prices for products. The same exponential behavior is observed after computer simulations for these models are allowed to evolve [1]. Even more complex dynamics, such as a firm-based framework where capital is borrowed and returned with interest while paying wages to multiple agents from profits, converge to the Boltzmann-Gibss distribution. 

One can show that the Boltzmann-Gibss distribution arises as the stationary solution to system dynamics that respect time-reversal symmetry of transaction probability transitions [1],

$$
f_{[m,m'] \to [m-\Delta, m'+\Delta]} = f_{[m-\Delta, m'+\Delta] \to [m,m']}
$$

With $f_{[m,m'] \to [m-\Delta, m'+\Delta]}$ meaning the probability of agents with $m$ and $m'$ wealth to transition into states of wealth $m-\Delta$ and $m'+\Delta$ during the interaction, respectively. The Boltzmann-Gibss distribution is the only solution to the equation and, while it does not depend on the dynamical rules of the models, the individual transition probabilities may very well do.

Proportional money transfer models where the transferred amount is taken to be a fixed fraction of the wealth ($\Delta m = \gamma m_i$) of the providing agent do not follow the Boltzmann-Gibbs distribution at equilibrium. This is model is called multiplicative asset exchange model. It has been shown this model almost follows a Gamma distribution [1]. Models where agents save money through a saving propensity variable $\lambda$ also follow Gamma distributions and the thermodynamic beta is found to be related to this saving parameter. 


# The Topology of Phase Space — Combinatorial Ledgers vs. Kinetic Drivers

### The Statistical Postulate of Shared Expectations
In an ideal closed asset-exchange network, the Maximum Entropy distribution (the Geometric/Bose-Einstein distribution) is derived entirely via static combinatorial partitioning,

$$\Omega = \binom{M + N - 1}{M}$$

This equation integrates over all possible universal states to establish a baseline expectation value $\mathbb{E}[n_m]$ for state occupancy. It contains no directional time arrows, no collision cross-sections, and no kinetic step sizes. It is a mathematical ledger of a fully randomized system.

### Universality and Kinematic Symmetries
The simulation serves as the master dynamic engine. Multiple, seemingly disparate microscopic kinematics can converge onto the exact same analytical ledger if they satisfy two foundational symmetries:

1. **Strict Local Conservation:** $w_i + w_j = w_i' + w_j' = M_{\text{pot}}$
2. **Microscopic Reversibility (Unbiased Mixing):** The transition probability matrix connecting any two accessible microstates $A$ and $B$ must satisfy $P(A \to B) = P(B \to A)$.

### Deforming the Baseline: The Value of Asymmetry
When an interaction rule breaks microscopic reversibility—such as the step-function distribution generated by continuous floor functions—the simulation naturally drifts away from the analytical baseline. 

Rather than viewing these deviations as code anomalies, this framework treats them as **Dynamic Symmetry Breaking**. The simulation acts as the definitive laboratory environment; when it leaves the baseline, it maps how local micro-level constraints (like transactional friction, asset holding rules, or geometric boundaries) can warp macroscopic inequality profiles away from pure thermodynamic decay.


## Fluctuation Dynamics & Convergence Tightness

While both `np.random.randint` (Discrete Uniform Split) and `trade_unitary` (Unitary Shift) theoretically converge to the Bose-Einstein/Geometric combinatorial predictions, they exhibit varying degrees of historical variance:

1. **Unconstrained Grand-Step Transitions (`randint`):**
   By allowing the entire local pool ($M_{\text{pot}}$) to be reallocated in a single trade step, transaction variance scales as $\mathcal{O}(M_{\text{pot}}^2)$ (from the variance of uniform distributions). These high-amplitude macroscopic fluctuations introduce statistical noise into finite-time snapshots, showing minor, transient deviations from the theoretical curve.

2. **Constrained Minimal Diffusion (`trade unitary`):**
   Restricting interactions to $\pm 1 \varepsilon$ fixes the transaction variance to unity ($\mathcal{O}(1)$). The simulation functions as an ideal local random walk across the discrete phase space shell. By eliminating high-volatility wealth teleportation, the system smoothly adheres to the maximum entropy attractor, yielding an exceptionally tight, low-variance fit to the exact canonical combinatorial predictions.

| Kinematic Protocol | Step Profile ($\Delta m$) | Trajectory Properties | Macro-Attractor |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `trade unitary` | Strict $\pm 1 \varepsilon$ | Smooth, localized random walk. Minimal fluctuation variance ($\mathcal{O}(1)$). | Pure Geometric Baseline |
| `randint` | Discrete uniform distribution over available integer space | High-volatility teleportation. Quadratic fluctuation variance ($\mathcal{O}(M_{\text{pot}}^2)$). | Pure Geometric Baseline |

Because both protocols respect the foundational symmetries, they share the exact same expectation attractor. The differences in their snapshot variances are not structural errors, but rather reflections of how each kinetic engine wanders across the invariant energy surface.


### Calculating the Intrinsic Variance per Domain Point

To quantify the magnitude of fluctuations in occupancy numbers assumed by the various statistical ensembles, we analyze the system from the "local" perspective of a single wealth level, $m$.

For any specific domain point, the allocation of $N$ agents can be conceptualized as a series of trials where an agent either possesses exactly $m$ wealth tokens (Success, with probability $P(m)$) or does not (Failure, with probability $1−P(m)$).

The explicit form of P(m) is determined by the geometric approximation for discrete economies as, which serves as the convergence distribution for the system as liquidity scales (for both the canonical and microcanonical ensemble),

$$
P(m) = (1-p)^x p \hspace{15pt} \textnormal{where} \hspace{8pt} p = 1-\left(1+\frac{N}{M}\right)^{-1}
$$

While the profile of $P(m)$ across the domain is governed by this geometric framework, the local variance profile depends fundamentally on the independence assumptions of the underlying ensemble models.

#### The Canonical Ensemble (Independent Allocations)

In the canonical ensemble, the system is open to wealth fluctuations via contact with a larger reservoir. Because the global wealth constraint is relaxed to an average requirement, the wealth states of individual agents are statistically independent.

Consequently, the occupancy $n_m$ at a specific wealth level $m$ is perfectly modeled by a Binomial distribution $n_m \sim \textnormal{Binomial}(N, P(m))$. The expected occupany and intrinsic variance are

$$
\begin{gather*}
\mathbb{E}[n_m] = N \cdot P(m) \\
\textnormal{Var}_{can}(n_m) = N\cdot P(m) \left(1 - P(m)\right)
\end{gather*}
$$

#### The Microcanonical Ensemble (Conserved Allocations)

In the microcanonical ensemble, the system is strictly closed. Both the total number of agents $N$ and the total wealth $M$ are rigidly fixed constants. This global conservation law breaks pure independence: if one agent shifts into wealth level $m$, it fundamentally restricts the resources available to the remaining agents.

The occupancy at wealth levels for an individual agent under the microcanonical view can be modeled using a multivariate hypergeometric distribution with the available objects $K_m$ for each wealth level $n_m$ mapped to the expected value of the occupation wealth level,

$$
K_m = N\cdot P(m)
$$

While this framing introduces non-local couplings and structural covariance penalties due to the simultaneous conservation of population and total wealth, a detailed derivation of the exact microcanonical variance is deferred at this stage. Instead, the current state of the project focuses primarily on the independent allocation framework of the canonical ensemble. An explicit analysis of the microcanonical occupancy variance, including its full finite-size correction terms, will only be addressed if required in future stages for modeling specific market dynamics.
